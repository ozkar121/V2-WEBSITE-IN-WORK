## Broker vs. operator: who is responsible for safety

It helps to understand a distinction many passengers miss. The **operator** holds the certificate to fly the aircraft, employs the crew, and is responsible for maintenance and insurance. The **broker** does not operate aircraft: it compares operators, negotiates, and coordinates the flight on the client's behalf.

This matters for one fundamental reason: responsibility for safety rests with the certified operator, not the broker. A serious broker never sells you "an aircraft"; it secures a **verified operator** for a specific mission. If an intermediary can't tell you who will operate your flight, under what certification, and with what insurance, you aren't buying safety —you're buying a risk you can't measure.

## The regulatory floor: AFAC certification

In Mexico, the civil aviation authority is **AFAC (Agencia Federal de Aviación Civil)**. Any operator selling charter flights must hold an **Air Operator Certificate (AOC)** under the air-taxi or non-scheduled air transport category. This certificate is not cosmetic paperwork: it requires approved operations manuals, an authorized maintenance program, operational control, and periodic oversight by the authority.

Three elements form the minimum floor every legal flight must meet in Mexico:

- **A current operator AOC.** AFAC's authorization to provide air transport service. Without it, the charter flight is illegal —no matter how new the aircraft looks.
- **The aircraft's Airworthiness Certificate.** The document confirming the aircraft is fit to fly and current on maintenance.
- **Correct registration and operation.** Mexican-registered aircraft (XA, XB, XC) fly domestic charter without issue. A US-registered (N) aircraft **may not** perform point-to-point flights within Mexico: that is **cabotage**, and AFAC restricts it. If you're offered a domestic flight on an N-registered aircraft, that's a regulatory red flag worth examining closely.

## The gold standard: international audits

AFAC certification is the mandatory floor. Above that floor are **voluntary** standards that set the most serious operators apart, because they mean submitting to independent audits that go beyond what the law requires.

- **IS-BAO (International Standard for Business Aircraft Operations).** IBAC's global standard for business aviation operations, structured in three progressive stages of safety maturity. An operator with IS-BAO has agreed to let a third party review its processes, its safety management system (SMS), and its operational culture.
- **IS-BAH.** The equivalent for FBOs and ground-handling companies. It matters because safety doesn't end in the air: a well-run ramp prevents incidents on the apron.
- **ARGUS and Wyvern.** US-based independent auditors widely used as a reference in international charter. Their ratings (for example ARGUS Gold, Gold Plus or Platinum, or Wyvern's Wingman) verify operator history, crew background, and safety processes. Not every Mexican operator holds them, but where they exist, they're a strong sign of professionalism.

No seal replaces judgment: an operator can be safe without IS-BAO, and a seal alone guarantees nothing if it's expired. But the willingness to undergo an independent audit says a great deal about the seriousness of whoever operates your flight.

## Insurance: the point almost no one checks

It's the least glamorous part and the most important. An operator can have everything else in order and still leave you exposed if its policy is insufficient. Before flying, confirm:

- **Third-party and passenger liability**, with coverage limits appropriate to the aircraft type and operation. For executive jets, limits should be measured in tens of millions of dollars, not symbolic figures.
- **Policy validity** for the exact dates of your flight, not a generic "for the year" coverage.
- **A certificate of insurance (COI)** you can request and, for corporate operations, the option to be named as *additional insured* where applicable.

A professional operator hands over its certificate of insurance without hesitation. If requesting that documentation produces evasions, it's the clearest red flag of all.

## Crew and maintenance

Behind every safe flight are two things invisible to the passenger: a trained crew and a well-maintained aircraft.

On the **crew** side, what matters is that pilots hold a current license, a *type rating* for the specific aircraft, up-to-date recurrent training and, above all, compliance with **duty and rest** limits. A fatigued pilot is a risk, and serious operators respect those limits even when it means rescheduling.

On **maintenance**, what matters is that the aircraft follows an AFAC-approved program, with current logbooks and airworthiness directives (ADs) and service bulletins (SBs) addressed on time. You don't have to audit this yourself —that's what your broker is for— but you do have the right to know someone did.

## Red flags: when to be wary

Some signals should set off alarms, no matter how attractive the price:

- They won't tell you **who** will operate the flight until the last moment, or ever.
- Evasions when you ask for the **AOC, airworthiness certificate, or insurance**.
- A price far below market with no clear reason (a legitimate empty leg justifies a deep discount; an improvised operator does not).
- They offer a **domestic flight on an N-registered aircraft** as if it were normal.
- Pressure to pay immediately, with no contract or cost breakdown.
- No one answers a simple technical question clearly.

Safety rarely fails all at once; it usually announces itself with small evasions. Listen for them.

## Checklist: what to ask before you pay

Before confirming any charter, demand a clear answer to these questions:

1. Which operator will perform the flight, and does it hold a current AFAC AOC?
2. What is the aircraft's registration, and is it correct for this operation?
3. Is the airworthiness certificate current?
4. Does the operator hold IS-BAO, ARGUS or Wyvern? At what stage or level?
5. What are the passenger and third-party liability insurance limits?
6. Can you send me the certificate of insurance for my flight dates?
7. Is the crew's type rating and recurrent training up to date?
8. Does the quote include a full breakdown (flight hours, ferry, handling, VAT, FBO)?
9. Is there a written charter agreement?
10. Who is my point of contact if something changes on the day of the flight?

If whoever sells you the flight answers these ten questions without hesitation, you're on the right track. If they dodge three or more, find another provider.
